02

Read the arrows first

The single rule that keeps you the right side of the Road Traffic Act.

Red arrow
Byway (BOAT) — drive it

Byways Open to All Traffic and unclassified county roads are the only rights of way you may legally drive. Even so, a BOAT can carry a Traffic Regulation Order — legal one week, banned the next.

Blue arrow
Bridleway — no cars

Horses, cyclists and walkers only. No motor vehicle right of way. Driving it is an offence, full stop.

Yellow arrow
Footpath — walkers only

On foot only. Never drive, and never nudge onto the verge or historic boundary banks beside any lane.

04

The Guide

Prep, technique, and the bits specific to a soft-roader with 77k on the clock.

Legal & courtesy

  • Drive only red-arrow byways (BOATs) and unclassified county roads. Nothing else.
  • Check the lane's live status for TROs — many are seasonal (some 4x4-restricted 30 Sep–1 May).
  • Vehicle fully road-legal: tax, MOT, insurance, plates, correct licence. Green laning is road use.
  • Don't drive lanes in very wet conditions — churning the surface is an offence and gets lanes closed.
  • Stay on the defined route. Never touch verges or ancient boundary banks.
  • Leave gates exactly as you found them.
  • Give way to walkers, cyclists and horses — pass horses at a crawl, engine barely above idle.
  • Single file, quiet, unhurried. Responsible use is the only thing keeping these lanes open.

Kit to carry

  • Rated recovery strap + gloves — and a second vehicle, or at least someone who knows your route.
  • Folding shovel and a pair of traction boards/mats.
  • 12V tyre inflator (essential if you drop pressures for grip).
  • Basic tools, cable ties, gaffer tape, a tow point you can actually reach.
  • OS Explorer map or offline GPS — signal drops out in the valleys.
  • Loppers for overgrowth, first-aid kit, water, warm layer.
  • Wellies — you'll be walking sections before you commit to them.
  • Check coolant and oil before setting off (K-series head-gasket watch).

Golden rule: walk anything you can't see the end of. Water depth, hidden ruts, a fallen tree round a blind bend — thirty seconds on foot saves a recovery, or worse, a drowned engine.

PACE

Slow as possible, fast as necessary

Let the car walk over obstacles on a whiff of throttle. Speed is not traction — it's just a bigger impact. Smoothness wins every time.

THE #1 SIN

Never spin the wheels

Sustained wheelspin digs you in and, on a Freelander, overheats the viscous coupling. The moment a wheel breaks free, back off. Don't power through it.

GEARS

Low gear, high revs, feet off

No low range here, so use 1st or 2nd and let the engine lug. Don't ride the clutch. On descents, engine-brake in 1st rather than stabbing the pedal.

TYRES

Drop a little for soft ground

Letting a few PSI out spreads the contact patch and finds grip in mud and sand. Only if you can reinflate — and pump them back up before tarmac.

THE LINE

Ride the crowns, straddle the ruts

Keep tyres on the high ground and the diff clear of the central ridge. Look well ahead, not at the bonnet. Place each wheel deliberately.

WATER

Assess before you enter

Walk it if you can. Enter at a steady walking pace to push a gentle bow wave, keep moving, never stop mid-crossing. The air intake sits low — deep water means a hydrolocked engine.

MUD

Momentum, not mashing

Steady and committed, minimal steering input. If you bog, don't floor it — reverse out along your own tracks and retry with a better line or lower pressures.

SLOPES

Straight up, straight down

Tackle gradients square-on, never at an angle. If a climb fails, don't turn on the hill — reverse straight back down in gear, under control.

CAMBER

Keep it level, lean uphill

Avoid side-slopes; they're where rollovers happen. If unavoidable, go slow, no sudden moves, weight to the high side.

RECOVERY

Stop before you're stuck

Feel it going and stop — don't dig a hole. Clear under the belly, use boards or a strap from a rated recovery point only. Never off a tow ball; they shear.

BRAKES

Dry them after water

Wet discs barely bite. After a crossing, ride the brakes gently for a few yards to squeegee off the water before you need them.

RESPECT

Leave no trace

Take litter home, don't widen the lane, report obstructions. Every churned-up byway is ammunition for the next closure order.

ACHILLES HEEL

The VCU

A sealed viscous coupling feeds drive rearwards. Prolonged wheelspin overheats it, and a seizing VCU can wreck the IRD/diff — a four-figure job. Gentle throttle, no sustained spin, ever.

DRIVETRAIN

No low range, no diff locks

Traction comes from the ABS braking a spinning wheel. Perfectly fine for byways; not built for axle-twisting extremes. Match the terrain to the tool.

DESCENTS

Use HDC if fitted

Hill Descent Control holds a crawl on loose downhills far better than your right foot. Let it work; keep off the brake.

CLEARANCE

Protect the belly and sills

Shorter approach/departure angles than a Defender. Pick lines that keep the bumpers, sills and diff off the ridges and rocks.

K-SERIES

Watch the temperature gauge

Low-speed slogging in summer loads the cooling system. Given the unknown head-gasket history, if the needle climbs, stop. (Worth getting that cluster backlight fixed so you can actually read it.)

WADING

Stay conservative in water

Modest wading depth and a low intake mean water is the Freelander's real enemy. When in doubt, don't — turn around.

AFTERCARE

Rinse underneath

Your MOT already flags brake-pipe corrosion. Grit and standing water accelerate it — jet-wash the underside and arches after every muddy run.

FIRST TIME

Go guided once

A single session with Surrey 4x4 Tours teaches the local lanes and shows you exactly how hard you can lean on the car before something expensive happens.

Links & clubs

Verified July 2026. The first two do most of the heavy lifting.

Essential · membership

GLASS + Trailwise2

The Green Lane Association. Membership (~£30/yr) unlocks Trailwise2 — the national map showing every legal BOAT and UCR with its current status. The single best answer to "can I drive this today?"

glass-uk.org Guided · bring your own

Surrey 4x4 Tours & Training

LANTRA-accredited training and guided lane trips for any skill level, most vehicles, max 5 plus guide. The low-risk way to learn the local lanes in your own Freelander. Meets near Box Hill.

surrey4x4tours.co.uk Official · legal status

Surrey CC — Vehicles & Rights of Way

The county's own guidance plus the downloadable list of BOATs and their Traffic Regulation Orders. Cross-check anything here before you commit to a lane.

surreycc.gov.uk Experience · their vehicles

Redhill / Salfords off-road day

Family-run centre near the A23 with a purpose-built course. Note: you drive their Polaris RZRs and buggies, not your Freelander — a fun day out, not a lane run.

intotheblue.co.uk Base · parking

Hindhead & Devil's Punch Bowl

National Trust car park and café — a handy staging point for the Frensham/Hindhead lane cluster. Paid parking by app or card.

nationaltrust.org.uk Base · refuel

Rykas Café, Box Hill

The classic biker café at the foot of Box Hill and the usual muster point near the Dorking lanes. Big free parking, proper breakfast.

rykas.co.uk Reference · SE England

Modern-Day Explorers

An enthusiast-run guide to byways, trails and pay-and-play sites across Surrey, Sussex, Kent and Hampshire — including the bring-your-own Sunday sites like Slindon and Boxgrove.

moderndayexplorers.net

Reality check: the Surrey lane network is heavily TRO'd and shifts season by season — Wolvens Lane is now permanently closed to 4x4s, and several surviving lanes only open to four-wheelers over summer (shut 30 Sep–1 May).
